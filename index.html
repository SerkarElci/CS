What are protocols and standards?

Protocol: the formal rules for how devices communicate — message structure, order, error handling (e.g., HTTP, TCP, IP).

Standard: a published specification (e.g., HTML, CSS, Unicode) ensuring that different vendors implement technology in a compatible way.

Protocols are the rules of communication; standards are the agreements to implement those rules consistently.

Key outcomes

  • Interoperability across browsers, servers, and networks
  • Reliable delivery and integrity of data
  • Security (encryption & authentication)
  • Scalability, portability & accessibility

Why protocols & standards matter

1) Interoperability

Common protocols (HTTP/TCP/IP) and standards (HTML/CSS/JS) allow any compliant browser to render any compliant site.

2) Reliability & Integrity

TCP handles acknowledgements, retransmission, and ordering so pages and files arrive complete.

3) Security & Trust

HTTPS (HTTP over TLS) standardizes encryption and server authentication for safe logins and payments.

4) Scalability & Performance

CDNs, proxies, and caches rely on shared HTTP semantics and headers to optimize delivery worldwide.

5) Portability & Compatibility

HTML/CSS separate content from presentation, enabling content to work on desktop, mobile, and assistive tech.

6) Accessibility & Internationalization

Semantic HTML and UTF‑8 ensure content is usable by screen readers and displays correctly in many languages.

Who sets and maintains them?

IETF

Publishes protocol RFCs like TCP/IP and HTTP; open, consensus-driven engineering process.

W3C & WHATWG

Define and maintain the web platform (HTML, CSS, DOM, accessibility). WHATWG maintains the HTML Living Standard.

ISO & others

Broader technical standards that influence ICT quality, safety, and interoperability worldwide.

IB exam guidance

Command term: Explain — give a detailed account with reasons/causes. Connect what a protocol/standard does to why it matters (interoperability, security, reliability, accessibility).

Model sentence: “Protocols and standards ensure that diverse systems interoperate using a common, published ‘language’. For example, HTTP defines how clients and servers exchange requests and responses, while TCP ensures reliable delivery and TLS provides security. This shared foundation lets any standards‑compliant browser load any standards‑compliant site securely and reliably.”
